a nutritionally-complete flatbread with a subtle flavour, which is low cost, as natural as possible, and has minimal impact on the environment

replacements

  • The almonds can be replaced with the same amount of sunflower seed kernels.
  • The flax seeds can be replaced with the same amount of chia seeds.
  • The carrot powder can be replaced with 4.6 g (2 tsp) of paprika, or 13 g (4 tbsp) of tomato powder.
  • The thyme, oregano, basil, and sage can be replaced with 2.2 g (¾ tsp) of spinach powder. The taste in this case is neutral, but it gives the result a green tint, and so for this reason this replacement pairs well with the carrot powder replacements above, particularly the tomato powder, as it helps to balance out the colour without really affecting the taste.
  • The duckweed is not required if the nutritional yeast contains vitamin B12.
  • The sundried mushroom powder is not required if the nutritional yeast contains vitamin D. It is also not required if you get enough sun exposure.
  • The kelp (also known as kombu) can be replaced with a gram of wakame, or by replacing the salt with iodised salt.
  • The ascorbic acid can be replaced with many alternative sources of vitamin C, including powdered acerola cherries and rose hips.
  • The wheat, both whole and plain, can be replaced with 80 g (¾ cup) of whole-grain oat flour or 80 g (½ cup) of whole-grain barley flour, then 260 g (around 2 cups) of some other flour(s), along with another gram (½ tsp) of nutritional yeast. A tortilla press, or two flat surfaces in general, can be more useful than a rolling pin when creating flatbreads that contain no gluten.
  • The nutritional yeast can be replaced by decreasing the plain flour to 160 g (1 and ¼ cups), increasing the almonds to 75 g (¾ cup), adding 42 g (½ cup) of ground sunflower seed kernels, and removing the sunflower seed oil. The texture is best in this case when they're made as stuffed flatbreads, where the almonds and sunflower seeds are used as the filling, along with anything else that you might want to add.
  • If the almonds are removed and the sunflower oil is increased to 33 ml (2 tbsp), then the end result will be close to completely healthy (though notably low in biotin) while being very cheap; the price will drop to around £1 a day, at which point the herbs can also be replaced with spinach powder, as mentioned above, to bring the price down to around 80p a day.
  • The choline bitartrate may be optional. The minimum amount of choline required in a person's diet is not well established, but it seems that this recipe exceeds the minimum without the choline bitartrate. The choline bitartrate is added to be on the safe side.

equipment

  • A cup and a mixing bowl.
  • A frying pan, and a hob or a hot plate.
  • A rolling pin, or a tortilla press, or two flat surfaces, or otherwise you can flatten them with your hands if necessary.
  • For many of the ingredients, particular the whole wheat flour and the ground flax seeds, it is best to grind them yourself, as their nutrition degrades quickly. For this, you can use a home mill (check to see if it supports grinding oily grains like flax seeds), a blender, or a food processor. This then means that you can store the ingredients in their whole form, improving their shelf life.

instructions

  1. Add the flax seeds to a cup with 60 ml (¼ cup) of water.
  2. Add the kelp to the cup in pieces as small as you can make and stir.
  3. Set aside around 10 g (1 tbsp) of the plain flour for use later when rolling.
  4. Add the rest of the dry ingredients to a bowl.
  5. When the flax seeds have sat for at least a few minutes, add them to the bowl with the oil and water. Don't add all the water at once, in case not all of it is required.
  6. Mix it all together with your finger tips, gradually bringing it into a dough, and knead it for a minute or so. If it is too wet and sticks to your hands, then add more flour; if it is too dry and doesn't come together, then add more water.
  7. Cover the dough and let it sit for around 10 minutes.
  8. Divide the dough in to 4–8 parts, depending on how big you want the end result. To stop them from sticking, dip each into the flour that was set aside earlier and roll them out until they're as thin as you want them, usually about 3–5 mm thick.
  9. Heat a pan on a low to medium-high heat, depending on how crispy you want them. A low heat cooks them slower, causing them to be crispier. Though note that too long on the heat will lead to nutrient loss. Generally, medium-high is a good choice.
  10. To see if the pan's hot enough, drop a few drops of water onto the pan; if the water quickly sizzles and evaporates, then it's ready.
  11. Cook each one until they're golden brown on both sides. It generally takes about 30–60 seconds for each side. There is usually no need to use oil, but you can add it to both sides if you prefer them that way.
  12. It is normal for them to puff up. When this happens, lightly press down on the puffed up area with a spatula so that they continue to cook evenly.
