replacements

  • The almonds can be replaced with the same amount of sunflower seed kernels.
  • The flax seeds can be replaced with the same amount of chia seeds.
  • The carrot powder can be replaced with 5 g (2 tsp) of paprika.
  • The duckweed is not required if the nutritional yeast contains vitamin B12.
  • The sundried shiitake mushroom powder is not required if the nutritional yeast contains vitamin D. It is also not required if you get enough sun exposure.
  • The kelp (also known as kombu) can be replaced with a gram of wakame, or by replacing the salt with iodised salt.
  • The ascorbic acid can be replaced with many alternative sources of vitamin C, including powdered acerola cherries and rose hips.
  • The wheat, both whole and plain, can be replaced with 80 g (¾ cup) of whole-grain oat flour or 80 g (½ cup) of whole-grain barley flour, then 260 g (around 2 cups) of some other flour(s), and another gram (½ tsp) of nutritional yeast. A tortilla press can also be more useful than a rolling pin when creating flatbreads that contain no gluten.
  • The nutritional yeast can be replaced by decreasing the plain flour to 160 g (1 and ¼ cups), increasing the almonds to 75 g (¾ cup), adding 42 g (½ cup) of ground sunflower seed kernels, and removing the sunflower seed oil. The texture is best in this case when they're made as stuffed flatbreads, where the almonds and sunflower seeds are used as the filling, along with anything else you might want to add.
  • The choline bitartrate may be optional. The minimum amount of choline required in a person's diet is not well established, but it seems that this recipe exceeds the minimum without the choline bitartrate. The choline bitartrate is added to be on the safe side.

equipment

  • A cup and a mixing bowl.
  • A frying pan, and a hob or a hot plate.
  • A rolling pin, although you can flatten them with your hands if necessary.
  • For many of the ingredients, particular the whole wheat flour and the ground flax seeds, it is best to grind them yourself, as their nutrition degrades quickly. For this, you can use a home mill (check to see if it supports grinding oily grains like flax seeds), or a blender, or a food processor. This then means that you can store the ingredients in their whole form, improving their shelf life.

instructions

  1. Add the flax seeds to a cup with 60 ml (¼ cup) of water, add the kelp in pieces as small as you can make and stir.
  2. Set aside around 10 g (1 tbsp) of the plain flour for use later when rolling.
  3. Add the rest of the dry ingredients to a bowl.
  4. When the flax seeds have sat for around 5 minutes, add them to the bowl with the oil and the water. Don't add all the water at once, in case not all of it is required.
  5. Mix it all together with your finger tips, gradually bringing it into a dough, and knead it for a minute or so. If it is too wet and sticks to your hands, then add more flour; if it is too dry and doesn't come together, then add more water.
  6. Cover the dough and let it sit for around 10 minutes.
  7. Divide the dough in to 4–8 parts, depending on how big you want the end result. To stop them from sticking, dip each part into the flour that was set aside earlier and roll it out until it's about 3–5 mm thick.
  8. Heat a pan on a low to medium-high heat, depending on how crispy you want them. A low heat cooks them slower, causing them to be crispier. To see if the pan's hot enough, drop a few drops of water on to the pan; if the water quickly sizzles and evaporates, then it's ready.
  9. Cook each one until they are golden brown on both sides. It usually takes around 30–60 seconds for each side. There is generally no need to use oil, but you can add it to each side if you prefer.
  10. It is normal for them to puff up. When this happens, lightly press down on the puffed up area with a spatula so that they continue to cook evenly.
